Your first affiliate dollar (the honest version)

Updated July 2026

Affiliate marketing is the simplest way a content channel makes money: you recommend a product, someone buys it through your link, and you earn a cut. No inventory, no support tickets, no product to build. It's also where most creators quietly give up, because the advice they follow was written to sell a course, not to describe what works.

Here's the version we'd give a friend.

Recommend things you'd recommend for free

The whole model depends on trust. The fastest way to burn it is to promote a product because the commission is high, not because it's good. Audiences can smell it, and one bad recommendation costs you more than one good commission earns. The tools we link to are the ones actually running this channel — that's not a marketing line, it's the filter.

If you wouldn't tell your friend to buy it, don't put a link on it.

Match the offer to the intent

Most affiliate content fails because it promotes a purchase to people who aren't in a buying mindset. The fix is to attach the offer to a moment where the viewer already wants the outcome. If a video explains how you made a voiceover, the link to the voice tool converts, because the viewer just watched it work. The content is the demo.

Practically: teach the thing, show the result, then mention the tool that produced it. In that order.

Pick offers that pay you for the long term

There are three shapes of commission, and they are not equal:

  • One-time: a flat cut on a single sale. Fine, but you're back to zero tomorrow.
  • Recurring: a cut every month the customer stays. Software subscriptions often pay this, and it compounds — last month's referrals still pay this month.
  • Lifetime / high-ticket: a large or ongoing share tied to a customer for as long as they buy.

Given the choice, weight toward recurring. A modest channel promoting a good subscription tool can build income that grows even on a slow content week.

Real example

The primary offers behind this brand include a recurring software tool (ElevenLabs, the voice engine) and a done-for-you affiliate framework. Both are linked from a single link-in-bio page so every platform points to one clean, trackable destination.

Track everything, or you're guessing

This is the step beginners skip and pros obsess over. If you don't know which link, on which platform, drove a sale, you can't do more of what works. The mechanics are simple: most affiliate networks let you append a tracking ID to a link. Use a different ID per source.

  • Bio links get one ID (say, tid=bio).
  • Video captions get another (tid=main).
  • A specific campaign gets its own.

Now your dashboard tells you a story instead of a number. If bio converts and captions don't, you move the offer. That single habit separates people who scale from people who plateau.

Disclose it, always

Affiliate relationships have to be disclosed — it's the law in most places and it's the right thing to do. A plain line does the job: "Some links are affiliate links; I may earn a commission at no extra cost to you." Far from hurting conversion, honest disclosure tends to help it, because it signals you're not hiding anything.

The first dollar is a systems test

Your first affiliate dollar isn't really about the dollar. It's proof the chain works: content reached the right person, the offer matched their intent, the link tracked, and the sale closed. Once that loop runs once, your job changes from "will this ever work" to "how do I run this loop more often." That's a much better problem to have.
